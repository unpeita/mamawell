## 1. Executive Summary

Japan loses an estimated ¥1.2 trillion annually to productivity loss and turnover driven by postpartum mental health disorders — yet no employer-focused digital solution exists. MamaWell is a B2B2C SaaS platform that enables companies to offer clinically-grounded postpartum mental health support to employees returning from maternity leave, reducing turnover while fulfilling Japan's growing health management obligations.

### The Problem
1 in 10 mothers in Japan experiences postpartum depression. The critical window — pregnancy through 12 months postpartum — coincides exactly with Japan's maternity leave period. When mothers return to work, they are often unsupported, leading to resignation, long-term leave, or silent presenteeism. Employers bear the cost, yet currently have no targeted digital tool to intervene.

### Our Solution
MamaWell is deployed by companies as an employee benefit. Employees access a mobile app offering:
- A clinically validated self-screening tool (EPDS, ISI, GAD-2)
- A postpartum-adapted digital CBT program in 3–5 minute daily sessions
- Warm referrals to medical professionals when clinically indicated
- Supervised by part-time psychiatrists and sleep specialists (2–3 physicians, Year 1)

### Why Now — Why Us
Japan's reporting requirements are expanding. DEI and return-to-work support are now board-level priorities. No competitor offers a postpartum-specific, employer-sold mental health product in Japan. Our team's key differentiator is an advisory connection to Prof. Yanagisawa Masashi (University of Tsukuba IIIS) — one of the world's foremost sleep researchers and discoverer of orexin — providing scientific credibility and a moat against commoditization.

### Competitive Advantage & Growth Strategy
- **B2B-first, then scale:** MamaWell enters the market through Japan's large enterprises, leveraging existing HR networks and the Kenkou Keiei certification framework to acquire the first pilot clients.
- **Proven model:** Globally, the B2B2C employer-paid model has demonstrated stronger unit economics and investor appeal than direct-to-consumer alternatives.
- **Uncontested white space:** No competitor currently occupies the postpartum-specific × employer-sold position in Japan.
- **Defensible moat:** MamaWell's combination of clinical specialization, Japan-native design, and an advisory connection to Prof. Yanagisawa creates a competitive position that broad-based platforms cannot replicate quickly.

## 2. Problem and Solution

### 2.1 The Problem
Postpartum depression and postpartum sleep disorders are the leading complication of pregnancy and childbirth in Japan. The causes are structural:
- Hormonal changes, sleep fragmentation, and caregiving load occur simultaneously
- Japan's medical and social system is designed around the baby — the mother's mental health is secondary
- Urbanization has eroded extended family support networks
- Social media amplifies anxiety and parental comparison

The patient journey reveals three critical bottlenecks:

| Bottleneck | Root Cause | Consequence |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Barrier to seeking help | Time scarcity + guilt about prioritizing oneself | Most mothers stop before ever consulting anyone |
| Medical vs. non-medical gray zone | Midwives and counselors cannot diagnose | "Wait and see" becomes the default |
| Treatment dropout | In-person therapy is expensive and time-consuming | Conditions deteriorate; resignation follows |

### 2.2 Why Current Alternatives Fall Short
- **General mental health apps:** not designed for postpartum realities (night feeds, infant sleep variability)
- **Standard CBT-I:** sleep restriction therapy is contraindicated for nursing mothers
- **In-person therapy:** logistically impossible for new mothers; waitlists of 2–6 months
- **Corporate EAPs:** low utilization rates, not postpartum-specific, no clinical escalation path

### 2.3 Our Value Proposition

| Product | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Screening & Navigation App | Short self-check using validated scales (EPDS, ISI, PSQI, GAD-2). Designed to reduce psychological resistance, avoid over-medicalization, and guide users to the right level of care. |
| Digital CBT Platform | Postpartum-adapted CBT program: no sleep restriction, designed around infant wake schedules, 3–5 min async sessions. Targets rumination and anxiety — not sleep quantity alone. Positions as a complement to (not replacement for) clinical care. |

## 3. Target Customers

### 3.1 Customer Segment (Who Pays)

| Segment | Rationale |
|---------|-----------|
| Large enterprises (1,000+ employees) | Primary target. Sufficient HR budget; under active pressure to demonstrate 健康経営 and DEI commitments. Estimated 12,000 companies in Japan. |
| Mid-size enterprises (300–1,000 employees) | Phase 2 expansion. Growing awareness of return-to-work risk; increasingly in scope for 健康経営 certification. |
| Corporate health insurance unions (健保組合) | Phase 3. Large-scale distribution channel; aligned incentive to reduce mental health claims. |

### 3.2 End User Profile
- Mothers, 3–12 months postpartum (primary), pregnancy period (secondary)
- First-time mothers are the core early adopter segment
- Experiencing subclinical anxiety or sleep disruption but not yet connected to medical care
- Working at a company that offers MamaWell as a benefit

### 3.3 Why They Will Adopt
**For employers:** MamaWell converts the hidden cost of postpartum attrition into a measurable, manageable program. A single prevented resignation at a large company saves ¥5–10M in recruitment and re-training costs — many times the annual subscription fee.

**For users:** The app removes the psychological barrier of "is my problem serious enough to see a doctor?" It offers support that fits into a 3-minute nursing break, not a 90-minute therapy session.

### 4.2 Bottom-Up SAM Derivation
MamaWell's pricing model charges per enrolled employee — the entire workforce — not per active postpartum user. This follows the standard B2B benefit-as-access model (analogous to EAP or Slack: companies pay for access regardless of utilization rate). The SAM is therefore calculated at the company level:

| Assumption | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Large enterprises in Japan (1,000+ employees) | ~12,000 companies (SME Agency, 2023) |
| Share actively investing in maternal/DEI benefits (est. 20%) | ~2,400 companies |
| Average employees per company | 3,000 people |
| Monthly price per employee (PEPM) | ¥3,500 |
| SAM calculation (2,400 × 3,000 × ¥3,500 × 12) | ¥302 billion |

**Note on active users:** Approximately 55–100 employees per 3,000-person company are postpartum-eligible at any given time (Ministry of Internal Affairs, 2023; birth rate proxy). However, because MamaWell is sold as a company-wide employee benefit — not billed per active user — this figure does not affect the revenue calculation. It does, however, inform the value proposition to HR: the per-actively-postpartum-employee cost works out to approximately ¥35,000–63,000/month per affected employee, a fraction of the cost of a single resignation.

The 20% adoption share and PEPM price are working assumptions that will be validated through pilot pricing experiments.

## 7. Go-to-Market Strategy

### 7.1 Target Buyer
Primary decision-maker: HR/Benefits Manager at large enterprises. Secondary influencer: Occupational physician or public health nurse. Final approver: CHRO or HR Director with budget authority.

### 7.2 Three-Phase Rollout

| Phase | Period | Goals and Tactics |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| Phase 1: Seed Customer Acquisition | Month 1–6 | Secure 3 pilot companies. Leverage team's existing HR connections. Offer free or heavily discounted pilots (no procurement approval needed). Collect EPDS/ISI outcome data and user NPS. |
| Phase 2: Market Entry | Month 7–18 | 20 paying companies. Outbound to METI's ~2,000 Kenkou Keiei certified companies. HR webinars featuring Prof. Yanagisawa-supervised content as lead magnet. Leverage occupational physician referral network. |
| Phase 3: Scale | Month 19–36 | 50 companies / ¥840M ARR. Build dedicated outbound sales team. Partner with health insurance unions and EAP vendors as distribution channels. Prepare Korea/Southeast Asia expansion. |

### 7.3 Acquisition Tactics (Phase 1 — First 3 Companies)
1. **Direct approach via HR network:** Team members and advisors have direct connections to HR managers at large enterprises. Target companies active in maternity/DEI initiatives. Propose 3-month no-cost, no-commitment pilot.
2. **Kenkou Keiei outbound:** Screen METI's public Kenkou Keiei list for companies highlighting women's health/return-to-work. Send personalized emails to HR departments. Target: 50–100 companies/month outreach → 10% meeting rate → 5 meetings.
3. **Advisor-featured webinar:** Host free HR webinar "Postpartum Sleep and Return-to-Work" featuring Prof. Yanagisawa's research. Attract 50 HR professionals, convert to sales leads.
4. **Occupational physician referral:** Approach occupational physicians through team members' medical network. Physician-to-HR recommendations dissolve initial skepticism and accelerate procurement.

### 7.4 Sales Funnel (Year 1 Target)

| Funnel Stage | Target |
|-------------|--------|
| Outreach contacts (email + events + referrals) | 500 companies |
| Meetings booked (10% conversion) | 50 companies |
| Pilot agreements (40% of meetings) | 20 companies |

## 10. Risks and Open Questions

### 10.1 Key Risks

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation | Validation Approach |
|------|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Solution reproducibility: infant sleep variability makes outcomes hard to guarantee | High | Redefine KPI from "sleep resolution" to "mental stability and return-to-work continuity." Redesign CBT-I without sleep restriction therapy. | A/B test: app users vs. non-users at pilot companies. Track EPDS scores and postpartum resignation rates at 3 and 6 months. |
| Long enterprise sales cycles (6–12 months) | High | Design Phase 1 pilots as no-cost, no-procurement-process. Concentrate outreach around April and October budget cycles. | Measure time-to-contract for first 3 pilots. If >6 months, pivot to SMEs or health insurance channel. |
| Founding team lacks female/postpartum perspective | Medium | Recruit 5–10 postpartum women as user advisors. Co-design with midwives and public health nurses. Prioritize female hiring. | User-test prototype with 20 mothers at 6–18 months postpartum. Achieve NPS ≥ 70 and 4-week retention ≥ 40% before launch. |
| Medical liability/regulatory risk | Medium | Explicitly design "not a substitute for clinical care" disclaimers. Physician-supervised escalation protocol. Position as wellness product (not SaMD) in Phase 1. | Legal review by medical law specialist before pilot. Reassess SaMD pathway in Year 3 when evidence accumulates. |
| Imitation risk from large players | Low | Depth of postpartum CBT-I redesign and Prof. Yanagisawa connection are hard to replicate short-term. Japan-native corporate relationships create switching costs. | Monitor competitor product updates quarterly. If imitation signals appear, accelerate feature differentiation. |

### 10.2 Critical Open Questions and Validation Plan

| Open Question | Validation Method | Decision Criteria |
|--------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Will companies pay ¥3,500 PEPM for a postpartum-specific product? | Price experiment: present 3 price tiers (¥2,000/¥3,500/¥5,000) to different pilot companies and measure conversion rates. | Paid conversion rate ≥ 50% at ¥3,500 validates pricing. If < 30%, revise price or value proposition. |
| Will users maintain engagement beyond 4 weeks (3–5 min/day)? | Deploy MVP to 50 postpartum mothers for 4 weeks (free user research). Measure DAU/MAU and session completion rates. | 4-week retention ≥ 40% and DAU/MAU ≥ 20% is required before scaling. |
| Does postpartum-specific CBT produce measurable clinical outcomes? | Design small-scale RCT (n=60) in partnership with University of Tsukuba IIIS. Track EPDS and ISI scores over 6 months against control group. | ≥ 20% EPDS improvement vs. control group. Publish results and use as enterprise sales evidence. |
