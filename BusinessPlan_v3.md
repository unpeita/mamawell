## 1. Executive Summary

Japan loses an estimated ¥1.2 trillion annually to productivity loss and turnover driven by postpartum mental health disorders — yet no employer-focused digital solution exists. MamaWell is a B2B2C SaaS platform that enables companies to offer clinically-grounded postpartum mental health support to employees returning from maternity leave, reducing turnover while fulfilling Japan's growing health management obligations.

### The Problem
1 in 10 mothers in Japan experiences postpartum depression. The critical window — pregnancy through 12 months postpartum — coincides exactly with Japan's maternity leave period. When mothers return to work, they are often unsupported, leading to resignation, long-term leave, or silent presenteeism. Employers bear the cost, yet currently have no targeted digital tool to intervene.

### Our Solution
MamaWell is deployed by companies as an employee benefit. Employees access a mobile app offering:
- A clinically validated self-screening tool (EPDS, ISI, GAD-2)
- A postpartum-adapted digital CBT program in 3–5 minute daily sessions
- Warm referrals to medical professionals when clinically indicated
- Supervised by part-time psychiatrists and sleep specialists (3–4 physicians, Year 1)

### Why Now — Why Us
Japan's reporting requirements are expanding. DEI and return-to-work support are now board-level priorities. No competitor offers a postpartum-specific, employer-sold mental health product in Japan. Our team's key differentiator is an exclusive advisory agreement with Prof. Yanagisawa Masashi (University of Tsukuba IIIS) — one of the world's foremost sleep researchers and discoverer of orexin — providing scientific credibility and a moat against commoditization.

## 2. Problem and Solution

### 2.1 The Problem
Postpartum depression and postpartum sleep disorders are the leading complication of pregnancy and childbirth in Japan. The causes are structural:
- Hormonal changes, sleep fragmentation, and caregiving load occur simultaneously
- Japan's medical and social system is designed around the baby — the mother's mental health is secondary
- Urbanization has eroded extended family support networks
- Social media amplifies anxiety and parental comparison

The patient journey reveals three critical bottlenecks:

| Bottleneck | Root Cause | Consequence |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Barrier to seeking help | Time scarcity + guilt about prioritizing oneself | Most mothers stop before ever consulting anyone |
| Medical vs. non-medical gray zone | Midwives and counselors cannot diagnose | "Wait and see" becomes the default |
| Treatment dropout | In-person therapy is expensive and time-consuming | Conditions deteriorate; resignation follows |

### 2.2 Why Current Alternatives Fall Short
- **General mental health apps:** not designed for postpartum realities (night feeds, infant sleep variability)
- **Standard CBT-I:** sleep restriction therapy is contraindicated for nursing mothers
- **In-person therapy:** logistically impossible for new mothers; waitlists of 2–6 months
- **Corporate EAPs:** low utilization rates (3–5%), not postpartum-specific, no clinical escalation path

### 2.3 Our Value Proposition

| Product | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Screening & Navigation App | Short self-check using validated scales (EPDS, ISI, PSQI, GAD-2). Designed to reduce psychological resistance, avoid over-medicalization, and guide users to the right level of care. |
| Digital CBT Platform | Postpartum-adapted cognitive behavioral intervention (inspired by CBT-I, redesigned without sleep restriction for nursing mothers). 3–5 min async sessions targeting rumination and anxiety. Night-feed mode: 1–2 min micro-sessions, single-hand operation, dark mode, offline capable. |
| Partner Support Module | Guidance for partners/spouses on supporting postpartum recovery. Opt-in stress trend sharing between partners. |

## 3. Target Customers

### 3.1 Customer Segment (Who Pays)

| Segment | Rationale |
|---------|-----------|
| Large enterprises (1,000+ employees) | Primary target. Sufficient HR budget; under active pressure to demonstrate 健康経営 and DEI commitments. Estimated 12,000 companies in Japan. |
| Mid-size enterprises (300–1,000 employees) | Phase 2 expansion. Growing awareness of return-to-work risk; increasingly in scope for 健康経営 certification. |
| Corporate health insurance unions (健保組合) | Phase 3. Large-scale distribution channel; aligned incentive to reduce mental health claims. |

### 3.2 End User Profile
- Mothers, 3–12 months postpartum (primary), pregnancy period (secondary)
- First-time mothers are the core early adopter segment
- Experiencing subclinical anxiety or sleep disruption but not yet connected to medical care
- Working at a company that offers MamaWell as a benefit

### 3.3 Why They Will Adopt
**For employers:** MamaWell converts the hidden cost of postpartum attrition into a measurable, manageable program. A single prevented resignation at a large company saves ¥5–10M in recruitment and re-training costs — many times the annual subscription fee.

**For users:** The app removes the psychological barrier of "is my problem serious enough to see a doctor?" It offers support that fits into a 3-minute nursing break, not a 90-minute therapy session. Positioning avoids clinical labels — the app is presented as "postpartum conditioning support" (産後コンディショニング), not "depression treatment."

### 3.4 Co-existence with Existing EAP
MamaWell is designed to complement, not replace, existing EAPs:

| Scenario | Recommended Channel |
|----------|-------------------|
| Postpartum-specific support (3–12 months) | MamaWell (specialized screening + CBT) |
| General mental health concerns | Existing EAP |
| Clinical escalation from MamaWell | MamaWell → physician referral → EAP follow-up |
| Non-postpartum family support | Existing EAP |

MamaWell provides a co-existence guide document to each client's HR team at deployment, clarifying routing between the two services.

### 4.2 Bottom-Up SAM Derivation
MamaWell's pricing model charges per enrolled employee — the entire workforce — not per active postpartum user. This follows the standard B2B benefit-as-access model (analogous to EAP or Slack: companies pay for access regardless of utilization rate). The SAM is therefore calculated at the company level:

| Assumption | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Large enterprises in Japan (1,000+ employees) | ~12,000 companies (SME Agency, 2023) |
| Share actively investing in maternal/DEI benefits (est. 20%) | ~2,400 companies |
| Average employees per company | 3,000 people |
| Monthly price per employee (PEPM) | ¥3,500 |
| SAM calculation (2,400 × 3,000 × ¥3,500 × 12) | ¥302 billion |

**Note on active users — the defensible assumption chain:**

| Step | Figure | Source |
|------|--------|--------|
| Female share of Japan's employed workforce | ~45% | Ministry of Internal Affairs, 2023 |
| Female employees in a 3,000-person company | ~1,350 | Derived |
| Share in child-bearing age (20–39) | ~40% of female employees | Estimated from age distribution |
| Postpartum-eligible in any given year (~birth rate proxy) | ~5–8% of that group | Based on ~700K births / ~8M working women |
| Active postpartum users at any moment (0–12 months) | ~55–100 employees per 3,000-person company | Derived |

Because MamaWell is sold as a company-wide employee benefit — not billed per active user — this figure does not affect the revenue calculation. It does inform the value proposition to HR: the per-actively-postpartum-employee cost works out to approximately ¥35,000–63,000/month per affected employee, a fraction of the cost of a single resignation (¥5–10M).

The 20% adoption share and PEPM price are working assumptions that will be validated through pilot pricing experiments (see Section 10.2).

3. **Advisor-featured webinar:** Host free HR webinar "Postpartum Sleep and Return-to-Work" featuring Prof. Yanagisawa's research. Attract 50 HR professionals, convert to sales leads.
4. **Occupational physician referral:** Approach occupational physicians through team members' medical network. Physician-to-HR recommendations dissolve initial skepticism and accelerate procurement.

### 8.3 Privacy Architecture

MamaWell handles mental health data classified as "要配慮個人情報" (Specially Sensitive Information) under Japan's APPI. The privacy design is a core product requirement, not a compliance afterthought.

| Principle | Implementation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Company sees NO individual data** | HR dashboard displays aggregate statistics only (department-level minimum 30 people to prevent re-identification) |
| **Data stays on MamaWell servers** | No personal health data is transmitted to or stored by the employer |
| **User controls everything** | Users can delete their data at any time; data retention period is user-configurable |
| **Opt-in from Day 1** | Research data consent is built into onboarding (not retrofitted), with separate consent for each use case |
| **Employee anonymity** | The employer cannot know which individual employees are using MamaWell; only aggregate usage counts are reported |

### 8.4 Key Execution Risks and Constraints
- **Physician recruitment:** Part-time psychiatrists and sleep specialists are scarce. Early relationship building with medical schools and the Yanagisawa network is critical.
- **Data privacy/compliance:** Health data requires APPI compliance. Legal review and PMDA pre-consultation are mandatory before pilot launch.
- **App quality and safety:** Clinical content requires validation. Internal review cycles with supervising physicians are mandatory before each content release.
- **Enterprise sales cycles:** Large company procurement takes 6–12 months even for free pilots. Phase 1 timeline is designed to account for this reality.

## 10. Risks and Open Questions

### 10.1 Key Risks

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation | Validation Approach |
|------|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| **SaMD regulatory risk:** EPDS screening + clinical referral may be classified as SaMD by PMDA | High | Initiate PMDA gray-zone pre-consultation in Month 1–3. Budget ¥15M/year for regulatory. Design product architecture to be SaMD-ready from Day 1. | PMDA formal response to gray-zone inquiry. If SaMD-classified, pivot to approval pathway (budget ¥300–500M for clinical trials). |
| **Solution reproducibility:** infant sleep variability makes outcomes hard to guarantee | High | Redefine KPI from "sleep resolution" to "mental stability and return-to-work continuity." Redesign CBT without sleep restriction therapy. | A/B test: app users vs. non-users at pilot companies. Track EPDS scores and postpartum resignation rates at 3 and 6 months. |
| **Long enterprise sales cycles** (6–12 months including security review) | High | Phase 1 timeline accounts for 6–9 month procurement. Concentrate outreach around April and October budget cycles. | Measure time-to-contract for first 3 pilots. If >12 months, pivot to SMEs or health insurance channel. |
| **Privacy/trust barrier:** employees fear company visibility into mental health data | High | Zero individual data exposure to employers (aggregate dashboard only, minimum group size 30). Third-party privacy audit. | User research: test privacy messaging with 20 postpartum mothers. Target: ≥80% trust the data protection after reading the explanation. |
| **Founding team lacks female/postpartum perspective** | Medium | Recruit 5–10 postpartum women as user advisors. Co-design with midwives and public health nurses. Prioritize female hiring. | User-test prototype with 20 mothers at 6–18 months postpartum. Achieve NPS ≥ 70 and 4-week retention ≥ 40% before launch. |
| **Medical liability risk** | Medium | Position as wellness in Phase 1 pending PMDA determination. Physician-supervised escalation protocol. 24-hour emergency referral pathway. | Legal review by medical law specialist before pilot. Reassess SaMD pathway based on PMDA response. |
| **Imitation risk from large players** | Low | Depth of postpartum CBT redesign, exclusive Yanagisawa agreement, and accumulated behavioral data are hard to replicate short-term. | Monitor competitor product updates quarterly. If imitation signals appear, accelerate feature differentiation and data moat. |
